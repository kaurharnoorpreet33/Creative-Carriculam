Creativity in the Curriculum

Early childhood education is defined as creativity, which is the ability to imagine, explore and convey ideas in a unique and meaningful manner. It enables children to experiment, make discoveries and look at obstacles in many different ways. Creativity enables young learners to develop curiosity, confidence, and resilience, which are important in their lifelong learning. In the contemporary early childhood context, creativity has become the core of holistic development, which involves emotional wellbeing, cognitive flexibility, social engagement and physical coordination (Kleibeuker et al., 2016). The Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF v2.0) places the role of creativity as an important aspect of children becoming confident and engaged learners, with imagination, investigation, and risk-taking being some of the critical aspects of learning dispositions (Australian Government Department of Education [AGDE], 2023).

Teachers are critical in supporting innovativeness. In the case of designing play-based, enquiry-led experiences, teachers develop an environment where children are able to explore freely and are not afraid of making any creative risks. The emphasis on the process instead of the finished product helps to think and express oneself more profoundly (Promoting Creativity in Early Childhood Education, 2023). Open-ended questions, routines modified to suit the needs of children with these two learning styles, easy-to-use materials, including loose ends and natural objects, and digital tools encourage children to make decisions and to experiment on their own. Facilitation involves the ability of teachers to create freedom without imposing strict direction but rather scaffolding learning opportunities without limiting imagination.

It is important not to consider creativity as an area of simply art or craft in the curriculum. Interdisciplinary models like STEAM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, and Mathematics) and STREAM (with the addition of Reading) encourage creativity as a connector in between enquiry and design thinking (García et al., 2025). These strategies promote questioning, testing, creating, and exchanging ideas in a variety of forms of representations, which promote critical and creative thinking. They also encourage teamwork and problem-solving that enables children to relate imagination and actual action.

Creativity has a complex nature emphasised in theories. According to the sociocultural theory displayed by Vygotsky, creativity is achieved by means of social interaction and dialogue as well as through play, with imagination being a developmental role. Constructivism as put forward by Piaget considers creativity as a way of recreating knowledge by exploring and experimenting. The theory of multiple intelligences according to Gardner can distinguish different types of creative manifestation – visual, linguistic, musical, bodily and interpersonal – and that creativity is not a one-dimensional concept. The newer studies point to the fact that creativity is correlated with self-regulation and executive functioning: children with the ability to plan, focus and adapt show better creative performance (Ghanamah et al., 2025).
